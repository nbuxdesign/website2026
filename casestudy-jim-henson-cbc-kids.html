Jim Henson + CBC Kids / Dotopedia

A fictional tablet app from a kids' show, turned into a real product for young explorers.

In Dot., the main character uses a fictional encyclopedia app to understand the natural world around her. Dotopedia was the real-life version of that idea: a kid-safe, tablet-first learning app that helped children explore, document, and build their own little encyclopedia as they went.

I led the UX for the app from concept into execution, working across a large team connected to The Jim Henson Company, CBC Kids, Industrial Brothers, and the production world around the show.

Role

UX Lead

Partners

The Jim Henson Company, CBC Kids, Industrial Brothers

Product

A wiki-like tablet app for kids ages 4-7

Launch

Tablet-first app with a smaller mobile experience

The starting point was deceptively simple.

Dot already had a strong idea inside the show: technology should help her get closer to the world, not pull her away from it. The fictional Dotopedia worked because it was not just a search tool. It was part encyclopedia, part field journal, part creative prompt.

The challenge was turning that into something real for very young kids. A normal wiki is dense, text-heavy, and built on assumptions that do not hold up when your audience may still be learning to read. Dotopedia needed the spirit of Wikipedia, but with training wheels, guardrails, audio cues, and a lot more room for play.

Make exploration feel safe

The app lived in a walled garden, so kids could browse, create, and revisit content without the open-ended risk of the wider internet.

Let kids contribute

The experience needed to support photos, sounds, short writing, and article creation so children could document their own world.

Keep nature in the loop

The product worked best when screen activity led back to real-world looking, noticing, collecting, and making.

My role was full-concept UX, not just screen production.

I worked on the product from the concept level through front-end design, shaping the major flows that would make the app understandable to kids, feasible for engineering, and true to the world of Dot.

That meant thinking through navigation, article structure, creation flows, activities, progress states, and the small pieces of instruction that help a young user understand what they can do next without feeling like they are being tested.

A wiki for kids is really a system of permissions.

For an adult, a wiki is mostly about access: search, browse, edit. For a child, the question is different. What am I allowed to touch? What happens if I make something? Will I break it? Is this mine?

Those questions shaped the UX. The product needed to make authorship feel possible without turning the app into a blank canvas. Kids could explore existing articles, unlock new content through play, and add their own media and notes. The structure was doing a quiet job: keeping the experience open enough to feel personal, but contained enough to feel safe.

Public launch features included

More than 200 articles, games and activities, stickers and Rangeroo Badges, article creation with photos, sound and text, multiple profiles on one device, and a walled-garden model with no social sharing.

The design problem was not how to make Wikipedia smaller. It was how to make contribution feel natural to a kid who is still learning how information works.

What shipped, and the world around it

Dotopedia launched as a tablet-first companion app with a mobile version as well. Public launch materials described it as a free app for iOS and Android, and the surrounding Dot. franchise had a strong reception in children's media.

The app itself went on to win the Youth Media Alliance Award of Excellence for Best Cross-platform Interactive Content, received a Common Sense Media 4-star rating, and was nominated for a BANFF Rockie Award, a Canadian Screen Award, and a Kidscreen Award for Best Branded Kids Learning App.
